Our Happy Little Network

Our domain name, ropine.com, is derived from atropine, the heart stimulant that makes "deadly nightshade" so deadly. (Atropine ... @ropine ... get it? Well, it seemed clever at the time.) All of the machines on our network, therefore, are named after plants in the nightshade (Solanaceae) family.

tomato

Thanks to the generosity of Andrew Greene, Heather Greene, Nomi Burstein, Michael Burstein, Jon Kamens, and Andrea Kamens, we are the proud owners of a Linksys BEFSR11 router and firewall. (They're geeks. We're geeks. They wanted to give us a house-warming present. What could be more appropriate?) Having this device between (most of) our computers and the DSL line will spare me a valuable learning experience.

horse-nettle

This is an ancient 486 machine that I salvaged from Unique Simchas. Before I got tomato, I was planning to use this machine as a firewall. It serves static Web pages with publicfile and lets us log in through ssh.

Using qmail, it accepts email to and from ropine.com. With rblsmtpd, it blocks mail from any IP address on the MAPS Realtime Blackhole List. (Since my unmunged email address appears in my Slashdot user profile, my Usenet postings, etc., I'm an easy target for the address harvesters. Installing this filter reduced incoming spam by at least two-thirds.)

This machine also runs dnscache, so it can be a caching proxy server for my other machines, and tinydns, so it can be the primary nameserver for ropine.com. (Until the appropriate switches are turned at my ISP and at Network Solutions, however, the authoritative ropine.com DNS servers are at ShoreNet.)

In order to provide DNS service to the rest of the Net, horse-nettle can't run within the firewall (tomato's implementation of NAT doesn't Do The Right Thing with inbound DNS queries). To reduce its exposure to 31337 h@x0rs, horse-nettle runs OpenBSD, the OS of choice for paranoid geeks on low budgets.

petunia

This is my workstation, a 350 MHz Pentium II, which we bought in July 1999. The retailer, by the way, was PCs for Everyone -- if you're in the Boston area and want a custom-built x86 computer, I strongly recommend them.

One of the features that distinguishes petunia from its neighbors is the case: after sliding it off, I can reattach it without wrestling a square meter of metal. Maybe the name-brand PC boxes are equally well-constructed, but eggplant and horse-nettle were assembled by NPC (a shop in Newton) and DTK Computer (I've never heard of them, either), respectively, and they obviously cut corners on the quality of their cases.

It runs Red Hat Linux 6.2, as well as PostgreSQL; one of my short-term goals is to install Apache as a Web server for dynamic pages, and then practice mucking around with mod_perl, Web/database integration, etc.

eggplant

Jen got this computer, a Pentium 100, back in 1996. After having defenestrated Windows 95, it now runs Red Hat Linux 6.0. Aside from the usual mailing and surfing, she runs StarOffice 5.1 on this machine to track, among other things, our household budget, which has no money left for new hardware. Oh, the pain, the suffering.
