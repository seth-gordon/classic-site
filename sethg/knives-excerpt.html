Knives [excerpt]
by Seth Gordon

According to my memory, from my earliest childhood through Sunday night, English was one language. On Monday morning, my first day of high school, there was boytalk and girltalk. I didn’t realize girltalk existed until my friend Mimi Rosenberg started speaking it.

I saw Mimi at the doorway to room 106, the homeroom across from mine. She had her left arm around another Asian girl, shorter and thinner, with a scar along her left cheek. With her right arm extended in front of her, Mimi took their picture with a cell phone. After the flash went off, she waved me closer. The phone buckled in her grip, like a thin black sponge, as she stuffed it in her pocket. She wore a Bowie knife in a leather holster against her jeans.

(The knife business started Sunday night. After I cut my hand, I noticed that my mother had a kaiken dagger strapped to the waist of her skirt: the scabbard and hilt combined to make a rectangle of white lacquer that I recognized from samurai movies.)

“Troy!” she squealed. “Penelope Ito, here, just moved in from London. Penelope, Troy Yamamoto, who just spent a month in Japan.”

Penelope nodded at me, rustling hair that went way down her back. A hilt the color of rope was tucked into her navy-blue dress. The scar traced a thin curve from her cheek to the hinge of her jaw. “Hi,” she said. Then she asked Mimi, “Do you go out with him?”

“No,” Mimi laughed, “we just grittick.”

“We what?” I asked.

“That’s just a girltalk word.” Mimi let go of Penelope’s shoulders and made a dismissive gesture, but her self-confidence seemed to waver.

Mimi and I had been friends since kindergarten, and I told anyone rude enough to ask (my little sister, for example) that I liked my classmate, but Not That Way. I assumed she felt the same way about me, but I wondered why she would talk about it in code.

“At age three,” Penelope said, “I played this game with my dad, where he pretended to understand girltalk. I would say ‘I veschang want a biscuit, Daddy!’ and he’d say ‘I papapopo have a biscuit, Penny!’ and we both just cracked up.”

I went over the words grittick and veschang in my head, so I could remember to look them up later; in Japan I had gotten used to picking up new vocabulary without pestering my new friends, what does that mean? what does that mean? Then I noticed the silence and realized that the girls were waiting for me to say something.

“How do you like America?”

Penelope looked around the hallway: glazed yellow brick walls, flourescent lights, room numbers hand-painted in black next to each doorway. I noticed a symbol like a hieroglyph under each room number: below 106 was a giant eye, the pupil looking up and to my left, while 105, my homeroom across the hall, had a sketched figure on horseback, arms upraised. Each symbol was framed by the mortar of a single brick.

“America suits me,” Penelope said.

I asked the least banal question I could come up with, and the one that was most on my mind. “What happened to your face?” I touched my own cheek with my right hand; feeling self-conscious about my own scar, I pressed my left knuckles into my thigh.

“I fought,” she said. “In my old school.” She looked at the floor, and even before Mimi gave me one of her sour looks, I knew I had said the wrong thing.

Before I could apologize or think of anything else to say, Mimi put a hand on the small of Penelope’s back. “Come on,” she said. “I can subcline for you after we sit down.” That was girltalk, as of Monday morning.

I could only retreat to my own homeroom. While I waited for the bell to ring, I picked a square of red paper out of my backpack and started folding. The origami book Haruka had given me in Japan had a design for a delivery truck, with little downward-pointing triangles for wheels, but I didn’t have the book with me and I was having trouble remembering all the folds. As I reflattened the paper, the bell rang, and Arnold Ross, another friend from eighth grade, sat down next to me. I expected him to say something about the origami, but instead he asked, “What happened to your hand?”

I flexed my left hand and made the scar along the knuckles wiggle. “I cut it.”

Arnold, who usually has the loudest voice in the room, practically whispered his response. “You cut it? With a blade?”

“It was just pizza,” I said sheepishly. “Two of the pieces were stuck together, you know, with the cheese,” and I pressed my hands against each other to demonstrate, “and I just was trying to cut them apart, and then Mom yelled at me, and startled me. It’s no big deal.” I couldn’t tell if Arnold’s expression meant you have impressed me or you have weirded me out.
