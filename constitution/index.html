Re-Reconstruction Amendment

a work of constitutional fanfic

Resolved by the Senate and the House of Representatives of the United States of America in Congress assembled (two-thirds of each House concurring therein), That the following article is proposed as an amendment to the Constitution of the United States, which shall be valid to all intents and purposes as part of the Constitution when ratified by the legislatures of three-fourths of the several States after the date of its submission for ratification:

Reorganization of the Legislative Branch

The House of Representatives

In an influential 1990 paper, Juan J. Linz argued that parliamentary democracies are more stable than presidential democracies. One reason for the greater stability, he said, is that in a presidential system, both the president and the legislature can claim popular mandates, and when they are in conflict, there is no clear way to decide who represents the “true” voice of the people. In 1990, Linz had to admit that the United States, as a long-lasting stable presidential democracy, was an exception to his rule; in 2025, alas, we seem much less exceptional. This proposal for constitutional reform is inspired by his observation. Elections to the House of Representatives shall be held on a date chosen by the President, as prescribed by law, which must be within six months of the dissolution of the previous House. Every citizen of the United States who is of eighteen years of age or older shall have the right to vote in these elections in their state and district of residence.

In the apportionment of Representatives, the number of persons in a state corresponding to a single Representative shall not be greater than the population of the least populous state.

Representatives shall be elected at large from each State, as follows: Each candidate for the House of Representatives shall be affiliated with a political party, with each independent candidate counted as a party of one. The standards regulating such affiliation shall be set by the laws of the United States and the several states. Within each State, seats in the House shall be allocated to each party in proportion to the votes for it, and the candidates with the most votes from each party shall be selected to fill those seats. This is known as open list proportional representation. It is used in Finland, Latvia, and Brazil. This technique gives racial minorities, regional interests, and ideological factions a route to representation without being blocked by gerrymandering or by party machinery.

The House must be dissolved when Congress refuses to pass a revenue bill requested by the Cabinet; or when it refuses to pass some other bill which the Cabinet has declared to be essential to the operation of the Government; or when the House declares that it has no confidence in the Cabinet; or when the President, on the advice of the Cabinet, requests that the House be dissolved; or when five years have passed since the most recent term of the House began.

Except as otherwise specified in this Article or in the law, the House shall have the exclusive power to consent to the appointment of ambassadors, public ministers and consuls, judges of the Supreme Court, and all other officers of the United States. The House shall also have the exclusive power to ratify treaties.

The Senate

Senators shall be chosen by the executive officers of their respective states, The Senate, in this model, is less democratic than the current actual Senate, but it is also much less powerful. In the best case, it can still function as the “cooling saucer” that George Washington (apocryphally) described. In the worst case, it cannot actually prevent the House from getting things done. in whatever manner is determined by the laws of that state, for terms of twelve years. They shall remain divided into three classes, such that one class of Senators may be chosen every fourth year.

The Senate shall have the power to appoint auditors, Inspectors General, and Special Prosecutors. Such officers, once appointed, may only be dismissed for cause, according to the terms of their commissions. They may have such authority to prosecute civil and criminal cases as the law permits.

If an officer of the United States exceeds their constitutional authority by accepting improper emoluments, or by holding office in violation of section 3 of the Fourteenth Amendment, then a Special Prosecutor appointed by the Senate may bring suit to deprive them of such emoluments or dismiss them from office.

Passage of Laws

A bill shall be signed by the President and become law when it is passed by both the House and the Senate; or when it is passed twice by the House, with at least one year (one month, in the case of money bills) intervening between the two votes. The British House of Lords functions in this way.

Treaties shall be ratified in the same manner that laws are passed.

Reorganization of the Executive Branch

The executive power of the United States shall be divided between a Cabinet and the President, both of whom shall be accountable to Congress. Officers of the Cabinet shall, before taking office, make the same oath or affirmation as the President. In parliamentary governments without monarchs (e.g., Ireland, Israel), the nearly-powerless head of state who fulfills a similar role is usually called the President. This proposal follows that model, although “let’s keep the title of ‘President’ but strip it of every power the President currently has” leads, admittedly, to some clunky drafting.

The Cabinet

A serving officer of the Cabinet may be classified as a confirmed officer, an interim officer, or a caretaker officer. Confirmed officers have been appointed or confirmed in their office by the House of Representatives in the current term of the House. Interim officers have been appointed by the President but not confirmed by the House. Caretaker officers were appointed before the most recent House elections and have not been confirmed or reappointed since then.

While the House is not in session, the President may appoint officers to the Cabinet to fill vacant seats and replace caretaker officers. In making such appointments, the President should exercise good judgment, choosing people who are likely to retain the confidence of the House. There is one corner case (as the geeks say) where a President could exercise significant discretionary power: If, as a result of inter-party squabbling, the House was deadlocked, it would not be able to form a majority to override whoever the President had chosen for the Cabinet.

When in session, the House may confirm interim or caretaker officers, or replace them.

The Cabinet must include at least five distinct persons: a Secretary of State, who shall direct the foreign relations of the United States; a Secretary of Defense, who shall act as Commander-in-Chief of the armed forces; an Attorney General, who shall represent the executive branch before the civil and criminal courts; a Secretary of the Treasury, who shall faithfully discharge the financial obligations of the United States; and a Secretary General, I should call this person “Prime Minister,” but I just couldn’t. who shall assume all executive obligations not otherwise specified, and who shall lead the Cabinet as first among equals.

The House may create or remove other Cabinet positions, and assign other executive responsibilities to each office, except as provided for in the following Section.

To replace a confirmed officer of the Cabinet, or to remove a Cabinet position held by a confirmed officer, or to reduce the power of a Cabinet position held by a confirmed officer, requires approval of either the officer affect, or of a majority of all House members duly seated and sworn. That is, not the “more yes votes than no votes” majority in usual parliamentary procedure.

The Cabinet may, by a two-thirds majority, expel one of its own officers. Such an officer, once expelled, may not be appointed to any Cabinet position until a new term of the House begins.

Members of the Cabinet may simultaneously be members of Congress, but may have no other private or public office.

The President

The President of the United States shall hold office for a term of seven years, and may not serve for more than one term.

Within six months of a new presidential term, or as soon as possible after the presidency becomes vacant, the House of Representatives and the Senate shall choose the President by meeting jointly as the Electoral College, in which each member of Congress casts a single vote.

The office of the President of the United States shall be ceremonial, ministerial, and non-political. The President shall only have such discretion as is explicitly granted by this Article, or by laws passed after this Article comes into effect.

The President shall sign all laws duly passed by Congress.

The President shall sign treaties, appoint ambassadors, and receive foreign ambassadors and other public ministers, upon the recommendation of the Secretary of State; commission officers of the armed forces upon the recommendation of the Secretary of Defense; appoint judges upon the recommendation of the Attorney General; make other appointments upon the recommendation of the Secretary General, or whatever Cabinet officer is empowered by law to make such recommendations. This eliminates the process for nominees to be confirmed by the Senate, but the experience of the last few decades suggests that there really is no procedural compromise between “the Senate shall courteously defer to the President’s choice” and “if the Senate is controlled by the party opposed to the President’s, then it will deny confirmation, because it can.”

The President has the discretion to commute a capital punishment under the laws of the United States to life in prison without parole; and, only upon application by the the Secretary General and at least one other officer of the Cabinet, may grant reprieves and pardons for offenses against the United States, except in cases of impeachment.

The office of Vice-President of the United States is hereby abolished. You won’t miss it. In the event of the President’s death, resignation, incapacity, or impeachment, the presiding officer of the Senate shall serve as acting President.

Reorganization of the Judicial Branch

The judicial power of the United States shall be vested in one Supreme Court, such Circuit Courts of Appeals as Congress has established or may later establish, and such inferior courts as Congress has established or may later establish. Each seat on the Supreme Court shall correspond to one of the Circuit Courts of Appeals, and nobody shall be appointed to the Supreme Court without having served for at least five years on the corresponding Circuit Courts of Appeals. Requiring Supreme Court nominees to have prior service on an appeals court is a small-c conservative measure: it makes it harder for the executive to make the judicial system change course at a radical speed. It also ensures that every Supreme Court nominee have some kind of track record dealing with controversial questions.

Congress may by law regulate the standards that a judicial candidate must meet before being nominated by the Attorney General.

Every two years, on a date to be determined by Congress, if no vacancies have been created in the Supreme Court during the preceding two years, the eldest member of the Supreme Court shall be obliged to retire.

Campaign Finance Reform

Congress and the States shall have the power to regulate and set limits on all election contributions and expenditures, including a candidate’s own spending, and to authorize the establishment of political committees to receive, spend, and publicly disclose the sources of those contributions and expenditures. This is lifted verbatim from an amendment proposed in 2011 by Sen. Bernie Sanders and Rep. Ted Deutch.

Amendments

Congress may, in the same way that it passes a law, propose a constitutional amendment to be ratified by referendum. On paper, the existing US Constitution is very difficult to amend: you need two thirds of each house of Congress and then three fourths of the state legislatures. In practice, there is a much easier path: find judges who believe that the provisions you want are already in the Constitution, when properly interpreted, and elevate those judges to the Supreme Court. Making the on-paper process easier might prevent interest groups from taking the under-the-table route of amendment. The several States shall vote on this proposal at least one year, but no less than six years, after Congress approves the proposal. Every citizen eligible to vote for members of the House of Representatives shall be eligible to vote in such a referendum. The amendment shall be ratified if it is approved by a majority of votes in the United States at large, and also by majorities of votes in more than half of the several States. This “double majority” requirement for a constitutional amendment is also used in Australia and Switzerland.

An amendment proposed by referendum may not deprive any State, without its consent, of its equal suffrage in the Senate.

Transitional Provisions

At the time this Article comes into effect, the sitting President of the United States shall become the Secretary General of the Cabinet; the sitting Vice-President of the United States shall become the Deputy Secretary General of the Cabinet; and the president pro tempore of the Senate shall serve as acting President until the Electoral College meets to choose a President.

The terms of the Senators and Representatives who are in office at the time that this Article comes into effect shall be extended as necessary to comply with the provisions of this Article.

All judges, Cabinet officers, and civil servants who are in office at the time that this Article comes into effect shall remain in office.

All laws passed by the United States and its subdivisions, and all debts and contracts entered into by the United States government, shall remain in effect, except that any clause empowering the President of the United States to make official acts shall be construed to require the consent of the appropriate Cabinet officer.
