The Treatment (excerpt)

by Seth Gordon

The men surrounded the groom as they danced on the beach, and the women surrounded the bride. As they paced out the steps of the macarena, the two phalanxes maintained a six-foot gap between them. Speakers above the high-tide line blasted music out to sea, a pre-Compulsion song about a girl wanting a new boyfriend.

Farther up the hill, behind the speakers, men huddled with other men to converse, and women with other women. When a man and woman faced each other, they kept two arms’ lengths apart, and raised their voices over the music and the April wind. One such woman called across the gap, “So, Bruce, are you bride’s family, groom’s family, or party crasher?” She wore a short black dress, tight enough to reveal her pregnancy, and leggings to match.

“Bride’s,” the man said, “sort of. Christine’s father is my adviser.” He buttoned his suit jacket against the cold. Bruce was a stocky, clean-shaven man, with a cleft in his chin, and black hair receding in a widow’s peak.

The woman, who had introduced herself as Liz, pursed her lips. “Adviser?”

“I’m a grad student in biochemistry, at MIT.” His necktie was striped red and grey, the MIT school colors.

“I knew you looked familiar,” she said, the pitch of her voice rising with excitement. “You were in the news the other week. Your lab is finding a cure for the Compulsion.”
