Scene: A smoke-filled room, somewhere in Washington, DC. ggg I suppose you're wondering why I brought you here. I would like to propose a change in the law which -- if you both help me achieve it -- will be in your mutual interest. bbb What interest could I have in common with a Bible-thumping Neanderthal? www What interest could I have in common with a Godless technocrat? ggg You have a common enemy: Hollywood. To you, Mr. Black, Hollywood threatens your livelihood: It litigates promising new technologies such as Napster out of existence, and expands its control over its customers through such laws as the Digital Millenium Copyright Act. To you, Mr. White, Hollywood threatens your values: Its products undermine traditional values, and its marketing campaigns appeal to customers' basest desires.

But Hollywood's money, and hence is political clout, depend on one thing: copyright. Without the legal right to impose licensing conditions on anyone who copies its works, your common enemy would be toothless. bbb Well, I'm not happy with a lot of the recent changes in copyright law, but I still believe in the moral principle behind copyright. If I devote my efforts to creating a book, or movie, or computer program, I deserve to be compensated for that effort. The people who say "information wants to be free" can do what they want with their own work, but they shouldn't make that choice for me. www And I don't see how eliminating copyright law will solve the problems that concern me. The French repealed their copyright laws soon after their revolution, and after they did so, the only publishers who could make money were the ones printing smut; that was the only kind of literature that had a sure enough return to be worth the investment. Eliminating copyright might eliminate Hollywood's marketing machine, but it would make all sorts of trash available for free. That wouldn't look like an improvement. ggg I don't want to eliminate copyright laws; I want to reform them. I would like you to work with me to draft an amendment to the copyright laws, which we can propose to our friends in Congress, and which contains the following provisions:

  • A tax shall be imposed on all digital-recording media, such as hard drives, recordable CDs, and flash memory, sold in the United States. (The procedure for determining the tax rate is discussed below.)
  • Revenues from this tax will not go into the general budget, but into a special trust fund.
  • Every year, the fund's administrators will survey a random sample of Americans, to find out (a) what digital-recording media they have access to, both at home and at work; (b) what work stored on that media has a registered copyright, where the copyright owner is not the person being surveyed; (c) how much storage space each of these copyrighted works is taking up.
  • Based on this survey, the trust fund will draw up an estimate of how many digital copies of each copyrighted work exist in the United States, and allocate the incoming tax money to the copyright holders, where each copyright holder's share is proportional to the number of estimated copies times the number of bits each copy takes up.
