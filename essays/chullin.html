Tractate Chullin, chapter 8

Mishnah 1

It is forbidden to cook any meat with milk, except for the meat of fish and locusts.

And it is forbidden to put it on the table, except for meat of fish and locusts.

One who swears off meat is allowed to eat the meat of fish and locusts.

This implies that poultry is forbidden by the Torah, according to one who does not hold like Rabbi Akiva. Because is not Rabbi Akiva the one who said, "Game and poultry are not [forbidden] from the Torah?"

But note the last part: "One who swears off meat is allowed to eat the meat of fish and locusts". We have arrived at the opinion of Rabbi Akiva, who said, "Any matters regarding which an agent would consult [the principal] are members of the category [given in the principal's instructions]". For it was taught in a Baraita:

One who swears off vegetables is allowed to eat cucumbers, but Rabbi Akiva forbids it.

They said to Rabbi Akiva: "If a man says to his agent, 'Get us some vegetables', might the agent not say, 'I only found cucumbers'?

He said to them "Just so. The agent would never say, 'I only found beans'. Rather, cucumbers are included in 'vegetables', but beans are not included in 'vegetables'."

The first part is according to the Rabbis, but the last part is according to Rabbi Akiva.

Rav Yosef said: "The mishnah is according to Rebbi, who quoted what he learned. Regarding oaths, he holds like Rabbi Akiva. Regarding milk and meat, he holds like the Rabbis."

Rav Ashi said: "All of the mishnah is according to Rabbi Akiva, and this is what he's saying: All meat is forbidden to be cooked with milk, whether it's defined as meat by the words of the Torah or by the words of the rabbis -- except for the meat of fish and locusts, which are defined as meat by neither the words of the Torah nor by the words of the rabbis.

And it is forbidden to put it on the table,...

Rav Yosef said: "Learn from this that poultry with milk is forbidden by the Torah. If it came into your mind that eating it was a rabbinic prohibition, that prohibition itself would be a fence around the Torah, and we prohibited putting on the table as a fence around eating."

But why should you say that we do not place a fence around a fence?

Because it was taught in another mishnah:

Challah outside of the Land is eaten with a non-Cohen at the table, and it is given to any Cohen who wants it.

[Rashi: "to any Cohen who wants it", even an ignoramus who is not careful about ritual purity; this is not so regarding challah separated within Israel....]

[Challah that is separated outside of Israel only has ritual-purity restrictions according to rabbinic law, as a fence. Challah separated within Israel cannot be given to Cohanim who are ignoramuses, as another fence. If challah separated outside of Israel could not be given to ignorant Cohanim, this would be a fence around a fence.]

Abayye said to him: "This is all well and good if we interpret 'challah outside of the Land' as meaning challah [that was separated outside of the Land and brought] within the Land of Israel, because there would be a reason to make a fence for that case, challah separated within Israel having Torah obligations associated with it. However, [regarding challah that is both separated and eaten] outside of the Land, since there is no reason to make a fence [there would be no fence around a fence]. But in this case, if it were permitted to put poultry and cheese on the table, people would come to put meat and cheese on the table, and violate the Torah prohibition of eating meat with milk.

Rav Sheshet challenged this: "Be that as it may, it's cold food with other cold food."

Abayye said: "There is a fence lest they put it in a chafing dish."

"Be that as it may, it's a kli sheni, and there is no cooking in a kli sheni."

"Rather, there is a fence lest they put it in a chafing dish that was a kli rishon."

Mishnah 2

"Poultry is placed with cheese on the table and not eaten", says the House of Shammai. And the House of Hillel says, "It is not placed on the table and it is not eaten". Rabbi Yosi says, "This is one of the leniencies of the House of Shammai and the stringencies of the House of Hillel." What table does this apply to? One at which people eat. But on a table where the cooked foods are being arranged, they put this next to that and nobody is concerned.

Rabbi Yosi and the Tanna Kamma [the anonymous author of the first part of the Mishnah] have redundant opinions.

Suppose you were to say that eating is the issue that they disagree on, since the Tanna Kamma is saying that [the Houses of Hillel and Shammai] are (a) arguing about putting things on the table, and (b) not arguing about eating. Then Rabbi Yosi says that eating itself is one of the leniencies of the House of Shammai and the stringencies of the House of Hillel.

But behold, it was taught in a Baraita:

Rabbi Yosi says: "There are six issues among the leniencies of the House of Shammai and the stringencies of the House of Hillel, and this is one of them: 'Poultry is placed with cheese on the table and not eaten', says the House of Shammai, and the House of Hillel says, 'It is not placed on the table and it is not eaten'."

Rather, this is what it's teaching us: the Tanna Kamma is Rabbi Yosi.

Anyone who speaks a thing in the name of the one who said it brings salvation to the world, as it is said:
And Esther spoke to the king in the name of Mordechai...
